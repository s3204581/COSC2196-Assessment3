Testing

Testing was conducted as part of each MVF (Minimum Viable Feature) which would be initially defined with a series of Use Cases to gauge user operability and customer value. This small-scale design scheme meant that testing was involved as part of the planning for viability and function of features so that the group could plan how to dedicate their resources. If, for example, a particular function was not aligning with the use case, the group could decide on its feasibility and potentially look at mitigation options.

Further testing of the prototype was also encouraged by all team members throughout the project lifecycle with any reported bugs noted down in an Issues Register. This artefact meant that the team could collaborate on the identified bugs, prioritise time, allocate resources and build a resolution strategy based off metrics such as the impact of the bug to the overall design.

Due to PhishNets inherent design which would invariably involve a user providing details such as Email account information, it was important to also research the types of security vulnerabilities that the application may be prone to and how best to mitigate these threats. An analysis of security threats and vulnerabilities was generated based on the OWASP Top Ten Web Application Security Risks which is available in the glossary. As the project draws closer to implementation of features such as secure login and information storage, the knowledge of risks such as broken access control, cryptographic failure and injection attack must be made available to the design team to implement the proper control measures and plan relevant, meaningful testing cases to support a viable product.
