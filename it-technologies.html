Autonomous Vehicles - Jake Harman

Autonomous vehicles, also known as self-driving cars and driverless cars, are vehicles that do not require human input to operate. They operate by sensing their environment using sensors, actuators, and machine learning systems to execute software, allowing them to effectively make choices on the road.

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) defines the 'level' of driving automation. There are 6 levels of driving automation as follows, split into two overarching categories with 3 levels in each.

Levels of Driving Automation

Levels 0-2: Human-Monitored Driving Environment

Level 0 - No Automation

This is the level of most vehicles on the road. At this level, the human makes all decisions while driving.

Also included at this level of automation are systems like Anti-lock Braking Systems (ABS) which assist after the human driver has made the choice.

Level 1 - Driver Assistance

This level encompasses systems that assist the driver in specific ways while operating the vehicle, such as adaptive cruise control.

Level 2 - Partial Driving Automation

Level 2 is the highest level of automation approved on Australian roads. Examples include vehicles with Tesla Autopilot and Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS).

Level 2 features include adaptive cruise control, lane keep assist, self-parking, blind spot monitoring, adaptive headlights, and traffic jam assist. Although the vehicle can perform steering and acceleration control, the human driver is still required to be actively aware and can take control at any time.

Levels 3-5: Automated System-Monitored Driving Environment

Level 3 - Conditional Automation

Level 3 vehicles are only allowed to be driven in certain parts of the world. The vehicle can make informed decisions but can request the human driver to take control if required.

Levels 4 and 5

Levels 4 and 5 are not currently publicly available in consumer vehicles. Level 4 technology is being used in some business capacities, mostly geared towards ridesharing services. Level 5 vehicles will not possess a steering wheel and represent fully autonomous vehicles.

It's anticipated that level 5 autonomous vehicles will not be available in the market for at least a decade.

Impact and Considerations

Autonomous vehicles have the potential to significantly impact the workforce, particularly in the taxi and rideshare services sector. The prevalence of autonomous vehicles is gradually becoming the norm, but challenges remain in terms of safety, liability, and defining responsibility in accidents involving autonomous vehicles.

In addition to passenger vehicles, autonomous technology is also being implemented in the trucking industry. While autonomous trucks offer benefits such as cost reduction and addressing driver shortages, adapting the technology to larger and heavier vehicles poses additional challenges.

As the technology progresses, there are cost considerations for consumers, with prices for autonomous features and vehicles being relatively high. However, the potential benefits include cost savings, reduced carbon footprint, and increased sustainability.

Furthermore, the implementation of driverless trucks may result in infrastructure-related job opportunities and potential roadworks and freeway closures as the technology is gradually adopted.
