Autonomous Vehicles - Jake Harman

Autonomous vehicles, also known as self-driving cars and driverless cars, are vehicles that do not require human input to operate. They operate by sensing their environment using sensors, actuators, and machine learning systems to execute software, allowing them to effectively make choices on the road.

The Society of Automotive Engineers (SAE) defines the 'level' of driving automation. There are 6 levels of driving automation as follows, split into two overarching categories with 3 levels in each.

Levels of Driving Automation

Levels 0-2: Human-Monitored Driving Environment

Level 0 - No Automation

This is the level of most vehicles on the road. At this level, the human makes all decisions while driving.

Also included at this level of automation are systems like Anti-lock Braking Systems (ABS) which assist after the human driver has made the choice.

Level 1 - Driver Assistance

This level encompasses systems that assist the driver in specific ways while operating the vehicle, such as adaptive cruise control.

Level 2 - Partial Driving Automation

Level 2 is the highest level of automation approved on Australian roads. Examples include vehicles with Tesla Autopilot and Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS).

Level 2 features include adaptive cruise control, lane keep assist, self-parking, blind spot monitoring, adaptive headlights, and traffic jam assist. Although the vehicle can perform steering and acceleration control, the human driver is still required to be actively aware and can take control at any time.

Levels 3-5: Automated System-Monitored Driving Environment

Level 3 - Conditional Automation

Level 3 vehicles are only allowed to be driven in certain parts of the world. The vehicle can make informed decisions but can request the human driver to take control if required.

Levels 4 and 5

Levels 4 and 5 are not currently publicly available in consumer vehicles. Level 4 technology is being used in some business capacities, mostly geared towards ridesharing services. Level 5 vehicles will not possess a steering wheel and represent fully autonomous vehicles.

It's anticipated that level 5 autonomous vehicles will not be available in the market for at least a decade.

Impact and Considerations

Autonomous vehicles have the potential to significantly impact the workforce, particularly in the taxi and rideshare services sector. The prevalence of autonomous vehicles is gradually becoming the norm, but challenges remain in terms of safety, liability, and defining responsibility in accidents involving autonomous vehicles.

In addition to passenger vehicles, autonomous technology is also being implemented in the trucking industry. While autonomous trucks offer benefits such as cost reduction and addressing driver shortages, adapting the technology to larger and heavier vehicles poses additional challenges.

As the technology progresses, there are cost considerations for consumers, with prices for autonomous features and vehicles being relatively high. However, the potential benefits include cost savings, reduced carbon footprint, and increased sustainability.

Furthermore, the implementation of driverless trucks may result in infrastructure-related job opportunities and potential roadworks and freeway closures as the technology is gradually adopted.

Cybersecurity - Hugo Ekert

Cybersecurity is a dynamic field dedicated to safeguarding computer systems, networks, and data from unauthorized access, damage, theft, and other malicious activities. Its primary objective is to protect sensitive information, including personal data, financial records, intellectual property, and government secrets, from a wide range of cyber threats. These threats manifest in various forms, such as hackers, malware, viruses, ransomware, phishing attacks, and more. Given the pervasive use of computer systems in critical sectors like banking and national security, successful cyber attacks can have severe consequences.

Current Cybersecurity Technologies

  • Firewalls: Network software that acts as a barrier between internal networks and the internet. A firewall has rules about the type of traffic that can be allowed through and filters out malicious activity assuming it has all the necessary rules. (What is a Firewall? 2023)
  • Antivirus Software: Software developed to detect malicious programs on a computer and remove or quarantine them.
  • Encryption: Mathematical algorithms that are used to distort data into an unreadable state that can only be read using a mathematical 'key'. Encryption is used for the safe storage of data so that even if the data is stolen, it cannot be read and used by the attacker. (What is encryption and how does it work? | Google Cloud)
  • Secure Sockets Layer and Transport Layer Security: Using encryption methodology, SSL/TLS is used to allow secure communication over the internet so that only the sender and receiver of packets can read their messages. If SSL traffic is intercepted by an attacker, they will not be able to decipher it.
  • Multi-Factor Authentication: Instead of just relying on a password for authentication, MFA requires another form of verification before allowing access. A common method of MFA is for a user to register a mobile phone number and be sent a code via SMS to verify. It is assumed that it is less likely for an attacker to have both the user's password and access to their mobile phone. (What is multi-factor authentication (MFA)?)

Emerging Capabilities in Cybersecurity

  • Artificial Intelligence: Artificial intelligence (AI) has become increasingly relevant and impactful across multiple industries, including cybersecurity. AI has the potential to revolutionize defense strategies by providing advanced capabilities in detecting cyber threats and analyzing network vulnerabilities. (Ravichandran, 2023)
  • Quantum Computing: Quantum computing has the potential to easily crack currently widely used encryption algorithms as the efficiency of processing with q-bits is much faster than regular bits. New algorithms will need to be developed that have a large enough key-space that even quantum computing will not be able to easily crack it. (Arel, Explore the impact of quantum computing on cryptography 2023)
  • Internet of Things (IoT) Security: As more things (fridges, televisions, Amazon Alexa, Google Nest) are embedded with devices that allow internet connectivity, the 'attack surface' for cyber attackers becomes greater. Standards will need to be developed for authentication and encryption of IoT devices. (Building Trust in IoT devices with powerful IoT security solutions)

Impact of Cybersecurity

Cybersecurity is a field that has wide-ranging effects, impacting everyone from everyday consumers to nation-states. For individuals, the likely impact includes increased security awareness and a greater need for personal cybersecurity measures. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) will become more prevalent, if not ubiquitous, as an additional layer of protection. Biometric forms of authentication, such as face ID, will also become more common and may be adopted by legacy institutions like banking.

As the collection and storage of personal data continue to increase, individuals may demand stronger action from governments to address data security concerns, particularly from companies that collect user data. This could lead to the implementation of stricter regulations aimed at ensuring a minimum cybersecurity standard for networks across various industries. Consequently, there will be a growing demand for cybersecurity professionals, not just in IT companies but across government and other sectors. This increased demand will contribute to the growth of the cybersecurity workforce. (Smith, 2022)

Furthermore, companies will face cybersecurity regulations and compliance requirements, necessitating robust security measures to protect their networks and sensitive data. Failure to comply with these regulations may result in financial penalties, reputational damage, and legal consequences. Consequently, organizations will need to invest in cybersecurity measures and employ skilled professionals to maintain a strong defense against cyber threats. (Medibank class action launched after massive hack put private information of millions on dark web 2023) (Information security manual (ISM))

Cybersecurity will increasingly emerge as a critical national security concern for nation-states. The protection of critical infrastructure will become imperative as state-sponsored hacking groups, backed by nations, target these assets. An example of this was seen in 2021 when a ransomware attack compromised a gas pipeline in the United States. (The attack on Colonial Pipeline 2023)

In the realm of international relations and geopolitics, cybersecurity has become an integral component. Nations now engage in cyber espionage, cyber warfare, and cyber influence operations to gain strategic advantages or disrupt adversaries. Advanced cybersecurity technologies can significantly enhance a nation's defensive capabilities, empowering it to safeguard its interests. (What is Stuxnet?)

Additionally, governments may establish initiatives to enhance cybersecurity education and training, promoting the development of a skilled cybersecurity workforce. (Online, 2023)

Machine Learning - Ben Wright

Machine learning is a broad term that refers to the use of algorithms, such as neural networks and evolutionary algorithms, to make predictions from data. Machine learning can be used in many different ways: for example, it can be used to predict future behavior (e.g., fraud detection), classify objects or people into categories (e.g., spam filtering), or even find patterns in huge amounts of data (e.g., image recognition).

Machine learning is a subfield of computer science that is concerned with the design and analysis of algorithms that can learn from data. Machine learning algorithms are usually applied to solve problems in which there is a large amount of unlabeled data, such as image recognition or speech recognition. The most common use cases for machine learning include image recognition, speech recognition, text classification, recommendation engines, fraud detection and credit scoring. In fact, many companies are using machine learning algorithms in their products today including Google Translate and Amazon’s Alexa voice assistant. Machine Learning is also being used by financial institutions like Credit Suisse Group AG (CSGN) for risk-based trading strategies as well as by insurance companies for claims handling processes.

The goal of machine learning is for an algorithm to be able to predict outcomes based on input features without being explicitly programmed. The most common way this happens in practice is via supervised training, where the algorithm receives labeled examples (for example, images with labels like “cat”) and learns how to classify new ones using those labels as inputs. In unsupervised training, the algorithm doesn’t receive any labeled examples but instead has access to all available unlabeled examples; it then tries to make sense out of them by predicting their class label itself (for example, whether they are photos or videos). Another type of supervised training involves having multiple algorithms compete against each other; one algorithm will be given a set of labeled examples while another gets its own set but also has access to all unlabeled ones so it can try different ways at classifying them. This competition helps identify the best performing model(s) for classification tasks in general — not just for specific types of data sets — because it allows us to see which models perform better under certain conditions compared with others when presented with similar amounts and kinds of data.

Machine Learning AI has been around since the 1950s, but it’s only in recent years that we’ve seen significant advancements in its capabilities. For example, when IBM Watson beat two human champions on Jeopardy! In 2011, or In 2015, when AlphaGo defeated Go champion Lee Sedol at the game of Go with an AI system developed by Google DeepMind called AlphaGo Zero. AI is a rapidly growing field that has the potential to impact every aspect of our lives in ways we can't even imagine yet. It’s been called “the next industrial revolution” by some experts, but it’s also been called “the most dangerous idea in history” by others because it could lead to robots taking over the world if they are programmed to do so.

The success of businesses such as IBM, Amazon, and Microsoft is apparent by the fact that they are beginning to rely on the ability of Machine Learning to automate processes and develop solutions for their various needs. With reliable insights and automated expertise, there may be no limit to what Machine Learning can help us achieve in the near future.

AI models such as ChatGPT represent a remarkable advancement in the field of machine learning. Powered by algorithms like neural networks, these models can understand and generate human-like text, enabling them to engage in sophisticated conversations and provide valuable insights. AI models are trained on vast amounts of data, allowing them to learn patterns, understand context, and generate relevant responses. These models have a wide range of applications, from customer support and virtual assistants to content generation and creative writing. They have the potential to revolutionize various industries by automating tasks, enhancing decision-making processes, and improving overall efficiency. However, as with any powerful technology, the ethical implications and responsible use of AI models are crucial considerations to ensure that they are developed and deployed in a manner that benefits society and respects individual privacy and security.

The potential impact of Machine Learning on both the technological industries and society as a whole is massive as almost every career or job opportunity can be optimized by the use of machine learning and artificial intelligence. The people who will be most affected by the use of machine learning and artificial intelligence would be people like engineers, transportation workers, health practitioners, administrators and even food service workers. One of the biggest industries where Machine Learning will have some of the biggest impacts is the healthcare industry. Using things like Artificial Intelligence to analyze and process patients medical data, healthcare professionals and doctors can achieve better and more accurate insights and diagnosis’ both much quicker than previously but also much easier than ever before. Some jobs however will not be affected (at least to a degree) as most lower paid service workers such as teachers and personal care employees are not the ideal candidates for being replaced by Machine Learning and Artificial Intelligence due to things like ease of employment, abundance of candidates and lower standards of hiring.

Machine learning has emerged as a powerful tool in the field of computer science, enabling algorithms to learn from data and make predictions without explicit programming. Its applications are wide-ranging, from predicting future behavior and classifying objects to finding patterns in large datasets. Major companies like Google and Amazon have integrated machine learning into their products, while industries such as finance and insurance are leveraging its capabilities for risk assessment and claims handling. Machine learning's potential impact is significant, with the ability to optimize various careers and job opportunities, including those in engineering, transportation, healthcare, administration, and more. While concerns about its implications exist, the advancements in machine learning hold tremendous promise, and with reliable insights and automated expertise, the possibilities for future achievements are limitless.

Clouds, Services, Servers - Dave Martin

What does it do?

Cloud computing is the delivery of computing resources and services over the internet. Rather than storing and processing data on local computers or servers, cloud computing is the networking of remote servers hosted on the internet to handle various tasks and provide access to resources. Cloud services include storage, databases, software applications, and networking. These services can be scaled up or down based on the needs of the user and traffic load, allowing for flexibility and cost efficiency. Users can access these cloud resources and services from an internet connection using a variety of smart devices such as computers or smartphones and from any location. The infrastructure of cloud computing consists of data centres that contain multiple servers and networking hardware. These data centres are managed by cloud service providers such as Amazon Web Services (AWS), who handle the maintenance, security, and availability of the infrastructure. Cloud computing offers a host of deployment models, such as Infrastructure as a Service (IaaS), Platform as a Service (PaaS), and Software as a Service (SaaS), allowing users to choose the level of control and management they require and highlights the elasticity and flexibility of this technology.

The future of cloud computing is likely to see continued growth and innovation with advancements in technology, evolving business needs and a constantly evolving digital economy. Organisations are increasingly adopting hybrid and multi-cloud strategies, leveraging a combination of public and private clouds to meet their specific requirements. This trend is likely to continue as businesses seek to optimise costs, improve performance, and enhance data security. As the Internet of Things (IoT) continues to expand, there will be a growing need to process data closer to the edge of the network and Edge computing enables faster response times, reduced latency, and more efficient bandwidth utilisation that cloud providers will require to provide support for.

Serverless computing, also known as Function as a Service (FaaS), is another innovative technology which allows developers to focus on writing code without worrying about underlying infrastructure management. It abstracts away the traditional server and infrastructure aspects, allowing for efficient scaling and cost optimisation. Serverless architectures are expected to become more prevalent as organisations seek greater agility and cost-effectiveness in the future. Cloud platforms are already offering machine learning services, enabling developers to leverage pre-built models and APIs for tasks such as natural language processing, image recognition, and data analysis. In the future, cloud providers are likely to enhance these capabilities further, making it easier for businesses to adopt machine learning technologies without requiring extensive expertise.

Quantum computing has the potential to revolutionise various industries by solving complex problems that are beyond the reach of classical computers. While still in its initial stages, cloud providers are investing in quantum computing research and development. In the future, we may see cloud-based quantum computing services becoming available, allowing organisations to access the power of quantum computing without the need for significant infrastructure investments.

Security and data privacy concerns have always been important considerations in cloud computing as threats to cloud systems highlight the negative implications to having limited access to physical hardware and the reliance of third parties monitoring and being responsible for a business's data. In the future, cloud providers will need to stay relevant and competitive by continuing to enhance their security measures such as advanced encryption, authentication mechanisms, and compliance certifications to address evolving threats that will also increase in quantity and quality in the future.

What is the likely impact?

The biggest impact this development will have will be the effects on the global economy as cloud computing requires constant improvements and efficiencies to networking and hardware infrastructure which means a major focus on information technology research and development. This drive for progress increases needs for consumption of resources, a skilled workforce, and a drive to create a product that is desirable to a growing consumer base to further economic growth.

With the increasing demand for computing resources, there is a growing focus on reducing the environmental impact of data centres and cloud infrastructure. This comes primarily through the energy required to power the data centres 80% of which is derived from fossil fuels and can have a disastrous impact to the environment. Cloud providers are exploring alternative ways to optimise energy usage, increase resource efficiency, and embrace renewable energy sources to create greener cloud computing environments and reduce their footprint.

Cloud computing has altered the job landscape by providing new roles and altering existing roles. It is important for workers to skill up and remain relevant by adapting to cloud architecture particularly as organisations look to shift from the traditional on-site data centre to a cloud infrastructure which can impact the data technician employee. There can be redundancies in the job of a traditional data centre technician or systems administrator as the requirement for manual, localised software and hardware upgrades and maintenance becomes less relevant with the shift to cloud-based infrastructure. The additional opportunities with cloud architecture however sees more requirement for specialised roles in cloud platform maintenance and support with additional opportunity to work remotely due to the anywhere, anytime nature of the cloud. In theory it is possible for technicians and administrators to learn and gain certifications in these new roles while still holding relevance to their previous work history.

How will this affect you?

This technology lowers the barrier of entry to any consumer by providing scalable solutions that can affect people that would typically be unable to access similar architecture and software development due to hardware costs and resource limitations. This is shown in the emergence of startup companies and individuals who can now bring innovation and ideas with how easy it is to build, test and deploy applications. The scalability of cloud computing provides a flexibility for users that can adapt to their requirements immediately and provides a fast and effortless way to store and backup data that eliminates the requirement of individual hardware, meaning that cloud services can cater to a wide variety of use cases. It also eliminates factors such as faulty equipment or theft which would have stopped users in the past and offers the added benefit of accessing personal data from multiple devices – an ability that could only be done by physical transfer previously. This access grants people the opportunity to be able to work together in real time on platforms such as MS Teams where collaboration and communication with online documents can be done collectively. This streamlines a lot of work and education applications as people can work remotely – a factor that has streamlined productivity and communications.

The affect that cloud technology has to a person in their everyday life such as a family member or friend is uniquely tied to its accessibility, convenience, and flexibility. The need for cloud architecture can vary drastically depending on the individual use case but the ability for the technology to scale to that use case requirement and provide a solution that caters to the individual highlights the power in scalability and ease of use. The affect can vary but the most common use case is data storage and backup – an efficient solution that highlights the importance of information technology and how it networks people together.
